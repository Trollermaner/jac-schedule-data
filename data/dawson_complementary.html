Anthropology

381-BWS-03
Introduction to Anthropology
1 section
  • Culture on the Move
  • 00001
  • Verity Whalen
  • This course is designed to introduce students to the social science of anthropology. An introduction to the major subfields of study including linguistics, biology, social and archaeology will allow students to understand how anthropologists study the origins and development of humans in the context of their physical and cultural environments. Students will learn about different cultures as well as the workings of their own culture. The concept of culture will be used to explore the movement of people, ideas and things. Motivations and impacts (socio-cultural, environmental, economic and political) will be examined to reveal complexities and nuances of mobility (or lack thereof) of people, ideas and things in today’s world.

  • Racism
  • 00002
  • Eliza Wood
  • While few people openly declare themselves racist today, racism—both individual and systemic—continues to haunt communities across the world. It rears its ugly head when we discuss “black on black crime,” mass incarceration, poverty rates, and school funding. We see its deadly impact in George Floyd struggling to breathe, Michael Brown’s body lying on the street in Ferguson, and Sandra Bland languishing in her cell. We watch as citizens and politicians openly espouse values that cloak their racism behind the veneers of “economic anxiety” and “traditional values.” We feel its presence when we see advertising, films, and media which under-represent people of color in a world that is mostly non-white. In this Contemporary Issues course, we will be engaging in a cross-disciplinary approach, using historical sources, literature, films, and other materials to examine the rise of race as a “scientific” concept and racism as a historical and present-day reality.

  • The course will explore Western approaches to education with a respect for Indigenous approaches, informed by the Mi’kmaq concept, “Two Eyed Seeing”.
